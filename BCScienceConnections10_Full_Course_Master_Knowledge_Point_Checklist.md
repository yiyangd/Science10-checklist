# Unit 1: DNA is the foundation for the unity and diversity of living things

## Chapter 1.1: How does an understanding of DNA help us investigate living things?

### 1.1 Concept 1: The variation in living things we see around us is due to DNA — p.10–11

- [ ] KP1: DNA as genetic material
  - Core idea/formula: DNA, or deoxyribonucleic acid, is a double-stranded nucleic acid that stores genetic information.
  - Key application: Use DNA to explain why organisms inherit traits and show variation.

- [ ] KP2: DNA and variation among living things
  - Core idea/formula: Differences in DNA help produce differences in appearance, structures, and life processes.
  - Key application: Connect DNA differences to variation among plants, animals, bacteria, protists, and fungi.

- [ ] KP3: Shared traits of life
  - Core idea/formula: Living things share basic characteristics such as being made of cells, using energy, growing, and reproducing.
  - Key application: Distinguish shared life processes from DNA-based differences among organisms.

- [ ] KP4: DNA and organism function
  - Core idea/formula: Most cells contain genetic information that influences an organism’s appearance and life processes.
  - Key application: Explain how DNA affects both visible traits and internal cell functions.

- [ ] KP5: DNA across major groups of organisms
  - Core idea/formula: DNA-based variation appears across many groups, including plants, animals, bacteria, protists, and fungi.
  - Key application: Use examples from different organism groups to show that DNA is connected to life’s diversity.

### 1.1 Concept 2: DNA is made of many nucleotides linked together in a specific order — p.12–13

- [ ] KP1: Nucleic acids
  - Core idea/formula: Cells contain nucleic acids such as DNA and RNA.
  - Key application: Identify DNA and RNA as large molecules built from smaller units.

- [ ] KP2: Nucleotide structure
  - Core idea/formula: A nucleotide consists of a phosphate group, a sugar, and a nitrogenous base.
  - Key application: Break DNA structure into its repeating building blocks.

- [ ] KP3: DNA nitrogenous bases
  - Core idea/formula: The nitrogenous bases in DNA are adenine \((A)\), cytosine \((C)\), guanine \((G)\), and thymine \((T)\).
  - Key application: Recognize the four DNA bases used to store genetic information.

- [ ] KP4: Double helix structure
  - Core idea/formula: DNA is made of two nucleotide strands arranged like a twisted ladder, or double helix.
  - Key application: Describe the sugar-phosphate backbone and base-pair “rungs” of DNA.

- [ ] KP5: Complementary base pairing
  - Core idea/formula: \(A\) pairs with \(T\), and \(G\) pairs with \(C\).
  - Key application: Determine the complementary DNA strand from a given DNA sequence.

- [ ] KP6: DNA sequence and protein instructions
  - Core idea/formula: The order of bases in DNA stores instructions that tell cells what proteins to make and how to make them.
  - Key application: Connect DNA base sequence to protein production and cell function.

- [ ] KP7: Proteins and organism function
  - Core idea/formula: Proteins make up much of cell and tissue structure and help control how cells form and function.
  - Key application: Explain how DNA instructions can affect organism development and body processes.

- [ ] KP8: Genome
  - Core idea/formula: The complete DNA sequence in each cell of an organism is called the organism’s genome.
  - Key application: Connect the human genome to about three billion base pairs distributed across 46 chromosomes.

### 1.1 Concept 3: DNA exists in chromosomes, which contain thousands of genes — p.14–15

- [ ] KP1: DNA, chromatin, and chromosomes
  - Core idea/formula: DNA exists as chromatin during most of the cell cycle and condenses into chromosomes during cell division.
  - Key application: Explain how long DNA can fit inside a small nucleus.

- [ ] KP2: Chromosome number in human body cells
  - Core idea/formula: Human somatic cells have 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs.
  - Key application: Identify normal human chromosome organization.

- [ ] KP3: Autosomes and sex chromosomes
  - Core idea/formula: Humans have 22 pairs of autosomes and one pair of sex chromosomes, usually \(XX\) or \(XY\).
  - Key application: Distinguish autosomes from sex chromosomes in a chromosome set.

- [ ] KP4: Homologous chromosomes
  - Core idea/formula: Homologous chromosomes have the same sequence of genes but are not identical.
  - Key application: Compare chromosome pairs by length, centromere location, banding pattern, and gene locations.

- [ ] KP5: Genes and alleles
  - Core idea/formula: A gene is a section of DNA that carries information for a trait; an allele is a different form of the same gene.
  - Key application: Explain how alleles contribute to differences in traits.

- [ ] KP6: Karyotypes
  - Core idea/formula: A karyotype is a photograph of paired homologous chromosomes arranged by size and type.
  - Key application: Use a karyotype to examine chromosome number, chromosome pairs, and sex chromosomes.

### 1.1 Concept 4: The structure of DNA is important to passing on genetic information — p.16

- [ ] KP1: DNA replication
  - Core idea/formula: Replication creates an exact copy of a DNA molecule.
  - Key application: Explain why cells must copy DNA before cell division.

- [ ] KP2: Genome copying
  - Core idea/formula: A cell replicates its whole genome once during the cell cycle.
  - Key application: Connect genome copying to accurate inheritance of genetic information.

- [ ] KP3: Template strands and complementary strands
  - Core idea/formula: Each new DNA molecule contains one original strand and one new complementary strand.
  - Key application: Use complementary base pairing to explain accurate DNA copying.

- [ ] KP4: DNA to RNA to protein
  - Core idea/formula: Genetic information passes from DNA to an RNA copy, and RNA directs the assembly of amino acids into a protein.
  - Key application: Trace how a gene can lead to production of a specific protein.

- [ ] KP5: Base sequence and protein sequence
  - Core idea/formula: The sequence of DNA bases determines the RNA base sequence, which determines the amino acid sequence of a protein.
  - Key application: Explain why changes in DNA sequence can affect protein production.

### 1.1 Concept 5: The different genetic make-up of organisms is reflected in the diversity of life — p.17–18

- [ ] KP1: Biodiversity
  - Core idea/formula: Biodiversity includes genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity.
  - Key application: Classify examples of biodiversity by level.

- [ ] KP2: Genetic diversity
  - Core idea/formula: Genetic diversity is the variety of inherited traits within a species or population.
  - Key application: Use inherited trait differences to explain variation among individuals.

- [ ] KP3: Gene pool
  - Core idea/formula: A gene pool is the sum of all versions, or alleles, of all genes in a population.
  - Key application: Compare genetic diversity within a population and within a species.

- [ ] KP4: Species and species diversity
  - Core idea/formula: A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed in nature and produce fertile offspring.
  - Key application: Describe species diversity as the variety and abundance of species in an area.

- [ ] KP5: Ecosystem diversity
  - Core idea/formula: Ecosystem diversity is the variety of ecosystems in the biosphere.
  - Key application: Connect biotic and abiotic factors to the variety of ecosystems on Earth.

- [ ] KP6: Genetic variation and biodiversity
  - Core idea/formula: Variation in genes helps support genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity.
  - Key application: Explain how genetic differences can contribute to the formation and survival of diverse life forms.

## Chapter 1.2: How is hereditary information passed from one generation to the next?

### 1.2 Concept 1: Genes pass on inherited traits from parent to offspring — p.26–30

- [ ] KP1: Genetics and heredity
  - Core idea/formula: Genetics is the study of heredity, or the passing of traits from parents to offspring.
  - Key application: Use genetics vocabulary to explain how inherited characteristics move across generations.

- [ ] KP2: Traits
  - Core idea/formula: A trait is an inherited characteristic, such as eye colour, hair colour, or flower colour.
  - Key application: Identify traits as observable results connected to inherited genetic information.

- [ ] KP3: Mendel’s controlled crosses
  - Core idea/formula: Mendel used controlled breeding of pea plants to study how traits are inherited.
  - Key application: Explain why controlled crosses and careful record keeping made Mendel’s experiments useful.

- [ ] KP4: True-breeding plants and generations
  - Core idea/formula: True-breeding plants consistently produce offspring with one form of a trait; \(F_1\) and \(F_2\) refer to first and second generations.
  - Key application: Track how a trait may disappear in \(F_1\) and reappear in \(F_2\).

- [ ] KP5: Law of segregation
  - Core idea/formula: Alleles for each inherited trait separate during gamete formation; each gamete carries one allele.
  - Key application: Connect meiosis, gametes, and fertilization to inheritance patterns.

- [ ] KP6: Dominant and recessive alleles
  - Core idea/formula: A dominant allele is expressed when present; a recessive allele is expressed only when two recessive alleles are present.
  - Key application: Use uppercase and lowercase allele symbols to represent inheritance.

- [ ] KP7: Genotype, phenotype, homozygous, and heterozygous
  - Core idea/formula: Genotype is the allele combination; phenotype is the expressed trait; homozygous means two same alleles; heterozygous means two different alleles.
  - Key application: Distinguish what an organism looks like from the alleles it carries.

### 1.2 Concept 2: Punnett squares show the probability of offspring inheriting specific traits — p.31–32

- [ ] KP1: Genetic cross
  - Core idea/formula: A genetic cross is deliberate breeding between a genetic male and a genetic female to produce offspring with genetic material from both parents.
  - Key application: Describe the parent genotypes involved in an inheritance problem.

- [ ] KP2: Monohybrid cross
  - Core idea/formula: A monohybrid cross studies inheritance of one trait.
  - Key application: Identify when a Punnett square should focus on one gene or trait.

- [ ] KP3: Punnett square structure
  - Core idea/formula: A Punnett square is a grid that shows possible offspring genotypes based on parent gametes.
  - Key application: Place possible alleles from each parent along the top and side of the grid.

- [ ] KP4: Genotypic ratios
  - Core idea/formula: A genotypic ratio compares the predicted allele combinations among offspring.
  - Key application: Express possible offspring genotypes as ratios or fractions.

- [ ] KP5: Phenotypic ratios and probability
  - Core idea/formula: A phenotypic ratio compares predicted physical traits; probability describes the chance of each outcome.
  - Key application: Interpret Punnett square results as probabilities, not guarantees.

### 1.2 Concept 3: Both alleles are expressed in codominance — p.33–34

- [ ] KP1: Codominance
  - Core idea/formula: In codominance, both alleles for a trait are fully expressed in a heterozygote.
  - Key application: Recognize phenotypes where two traits appear together instead of one hiding the other.

- [ ] KP2: Codominant allele notation
  - Core idea/formula: Codominant alleles can be written with one capital gene letter and different superscript letters.
  - Key application: Read and write genotypes such as \(H^R H^W\) for codominant traits.

- [ ] KP3: Roan animals
  - Core idea/formula: A roan animal shows both base colour and white hair because both alleles are expressed.
  - Key application: Use roan coat colour as a visible model of codominance.

- [ ] KP4: Sickle cell anemia and hemoglobin
  - Core idea/formula: Sickle cell anemia is linked to an allele that affects hemoglobin, a protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen.
  - Key application: Connect allele differences to protein structure, cell shape, and body function.

- [ ] KP5: Sickle cell trait
  - Core idea/formula: Individuals with genotype \(Hb^A Hb^S\) have both normal and sickled red blood cells.
  - Key application: Explain how heterozygotes can show both allele effects.

- [ ] KP6: Heterozygote advantage
  - Core idea/formula: In some environments, the sickle cell trait can provide resistance to malaria.
  - Key application: Connect inheritance patterns to survival advantage in specific environments.

### 1.2 Concept 4: In incomplete dominance, alleles are neither dominant nor recessive — p.35

- [ ] KP1: Incomplete dominance
  - Core idea/formula: In incomplete dominance, neither allele completely hides the other.
  - Key application: Identify inheritance patterns where the heterozygote has an intermediate phenotype.

- [ ] KP2: Intermediate phenotype
  - Core idea/formula: A heterozygote in incomplete dominance shows a phenotype between the two homozygous phenotypes.
  - Key application: Recognize blended-looking traits as possible incomplete dominance.

- [ ] KP3: Incomplete dominance ratios
  - Core idea/formula: A heterozygote × heterozygote cross can produce a \(1:2:1\) phenotypic ratio.
  - Key application: Use Punnett squares to predict all three phenotypes.

- [ ] KP4: Superscript notation
  - Core idea/formula: Incomplete dominance can use superscripts, such as \(C^R\) and \(C^W\), to represent allele forms.
  - Key application: Avoid using lowercase symbols unless an allele is truly recessive.

- [ ] KP5: Incomplete dominance versus codominance
  - Core idea/formula: Incomplete dominance gives an intermediate phenotype; codominance shows both alleles fully expressed.
  - Key application: Classify inheritance patterns by how the heterozygote appears.

### 1.2 Concept 5: Some inherited traits are due to alleles on the sex chromosomes — p.36

- [ ] KP1: Sex-linked traits
  - Core idea/formula: Sex-linked traits are controlled by genes located on sex chromosomes.
  - Key application: Identify when inheritance depends on \(X\) or \(Y\) chromosomes.

- [ ] KP2: X-linked traits
  - Core idea/formula: Traits controlled by genes on the \(X\) chromosome are called X-linked traits.
  - Key application: Track X-linked alleles differently in genetic males and genetic females.

- [ ] KP3: Recessive X-linked inheritance
  - Core idea/formula: Genetic males are affected by recessive X-linked traits more often because they have only one \(X\) chromosome.
  - Key application: Explain why some recessive traits appear more often in males.

- [ ] KP4: Carriers
  - Core idea/formula: A genetic female can carry a recessive X-linked allele on one \(X\) chromosome without expressing the trait.
  - Key application: Use carrier genotypes to explain how recessive X-linked traits pass through families.

- [ ] KP5: Red-green colour vision deficiency
  - Core idea/formula: Red-green colour vision deficiency is a recessive X-linked trait.
  - Key application: Use \(X^B\), \(X^b\), and \(Y\) notation to model sex-linked inheritance.

## Chapter 1.3: How can natural and artificial selection influence changes in populations?

### 1.3 Concept 1: DNA mutations produce genetic diversity within a population — p.46

- [ ] KP1: Mutation
  - Core idea/formula: A mutation is a permanent change in the DNA of an organism.
  - Key application: Explain how mutations can create new alleles and new genetic variation.

- [ ] KP2: Genetic variation in populations
  - Core idea/formula: Variation among individuals in a population is largely the result of differences in genes.
  - Key application: Connect inherited gene differences to variation within a species.

- [ ] KP3: Sources of mutations
  - Core idea/formula: Mutations can occur spontaneously during DNA copying or result from environmental agents.
  - Key application: Identify how DNA changes can arise before a cell divides or from outside factors.

- [ ] KP4: Effects of mutations
  - Core idea/formula: Mutations may be harmful, neutral, or beneficial.
  - Key application: Avoid assuming that every mutation is harmful.

- [ ] KP5: Somatic cell mutations
  - Core idea/formula: A mutation in a body cell disappears from the population when the organism dies.
  - Key application: Distinguish non-inherited body cell mutations from inherited genetic changes.

- [ ] KP6: Gamete mutations
  - Core idea/formula: A mutation in a gamete may be passed to future generations as a new allele.
  - Key application: Explain why inherited mutations are important for long-term population change.

### 1.3 Concept 2: Natural selection favours traits that make an organism better suited to its environment — p.47–49

- [ ] KP1: Selective advantage
  - Core idea/formula: A selective advantage is a genetic advantage that improves an organism’s chance of survival and reproduction.
  - Key application: Identify traits that help organisms survive under specific environmental conditions.

- [ ] KP2: Natural selection
  - Core idea/formula: Natural selection changes the characteristics of a population over many generations as organisms with heritable traits survive and reproduce.
  - Key application: Explain how inherited traits can become more common in a population.

- [ ] KP3: Adaptation
  - Core idea/formula: An adaptation is a structural, behavioural, or physiological feature that helps an organism survive and reproduce.
  - Key application: Classify examples of adaptations by how they improve survival or reproduction.

- [ ] KP4: Genetic diversity and selection
  - Core idea/formula: Natural selection requires variation within a species or population.
  - Key application: Explain why populations with genetic diversity have more possible responses to environmental change.

- [ ] KP5: Natural selection acts on populations
  - Core idea/formula: Individuals do not change during their lifetime; populations change over generations.
  - Key application: Avoid saying that an individual organism evolves during its life.

- [ ] KP6: Selective pressure
  - Core idea/formula: Selective pressure is an environmental condition that favours some traits and selects against others.
  - Key application: Identify abiotic and biotic selective pressures such as antibiotics, light levels, predators, parasites, and competition.

- [ ] KP7: Natural selection is situational
  - Core idea/formula: Natural selection has no purpose or direction; the usefulness of a trait depends on the environment.
  - Key application: Explain why a trait can be helpful in one situation but not in another.

### 1.3 Concept 3: Natural selection can lead to the formation of new species — p.50–53

- [ ] KP1: Species
  - Core idea/formula: A species is a population or group of populations whose members can interbreed and produce fertile offspring.
  - Key application: Use reproductive ability to distinguish whether groups belong to the same species.

- [ ] KP2: Speciation
  - Core idea/formula: Speciation occurs when members of a population change enough that they can no longer produce fertile offspring with the original population.
  - Key application: Explain how one species can split into two or more species over time.

- [ ] KP3: Geographic isolation
  - Core idea/formula: A population can be split by a barrier such as a glacier, lava flow, ocean-level change, or movement to a separate habitat.
  - Key application: Connect isolation to separate gene pools and different evolutionary paths.

- [ ] KP4: Adaptive radiation
  - Core idea/formula: Adaptive radiation is the diversification of a common ancestral species into differently adapted species.
  - Key application: Use island species, such as Galapagos finches, as examples of adaptive radiation.

- [ ] KP5: Mutations and natural selection in speciation
  - Core idea/formula: Mutations create variation, and natural selection can favour different traits in different environments.
  - Key application: Explain how isolated populations become increasingly different over many generations.

- [ ] KP6: Extinction
  - Core idea/formula: Extinction occurs when a species completely disappears from Earth.
  - Key application: Connect environmental change and selective pressure to the loss of species.

- [ ] KP7: Mass extinction
  - Core idea/formula: Mass extinction events are sharp declines in biodiversity caused by large-scale environmental changes.
  - Key application: Relate extinction events to major changes in ecosystems and species diversity.

### 1.3 Concept 4: Environmental factors can cause mutations — p.54

- [ ] KP1: Mutagen
  - Core idea/formula: A mutagen is a substance or event that increases the rate of mutation.
  - Key application: Identify environmental factors that can damage or change DNA.

- [ ] KP2: Physical mutagens
  - Core idea/formula: Physical mutagens cause physical changes to DNA structure.
  - Key application: Recognize X-rays and ultraviolet radiation as examples of physical mutagens.

- [ ] KP3: Chemical mutagens
  - Core idea/formula: Chemical mutagens are molecules that can enter a cell nucleus and react with DNA.
  - Key application: Recognize nitrites and gasoline fumes as examples of chemical mutagens.

- [ ] KP4: Carcinogens
  - Core idea/formula: A carcinogen is a substance or agent that causes cancer.
  - Key application: Connect ultraviolet radiation and cigarette smoke compounds to increased cancer risk.

- [ ] KP5: Mutations and proteins
  - Core idea/formula: A mutation can change gene instructions and affect the production of a specific protein.
  - Key application: Explain how DNA changes may affect cell function and organism health.

### 1.3 Concept 5: Humans select desired characteristics in organisms to be passed on to the next generation — p.55–57

- [ ] KP1: Artificial selection
  - Core idea/formula: Artificial selection is selective pressure exerted by humans to improve or modify desirable traits.
  - Key application: Identify selective breeding in crops, livestock, companion animals, and domestic species.

- [ ] KP2: Natural selection versus artificial selection
  - Core idea/formula: In natural selection, environmental conditions select traits; in artificial selection, humans choose traits.
  - Key application: Compare how traits become more common in natural and human-directed contexts.

- [ ] KP3: Artificial selection in animals

- [ ] KP4: Artificial selection in food crops

- [ ] KP5: Limits of selective breeding
  - Core idea/formula: Breeding for one desired trait can reduce tolerance for other environmental conditions.
  - Key application: Explain why crop breeding must balance productivity with genetic variation.

- [ ] KP6: Consequences of artificial selection
  - Core idea/formula: Artificial selection can have positive and negative effects, including health problems or reduced genetic diversity.
  - Key application: Evaluate benefits and risks of breeding organisms for specific traits.

- [ ] KP7: Monoculture
  - Core idea/formula: Monoculture is the repeated planting of the same varieties of a species over large areas.

- Top 5 Core Formulas:
  - Mechanical kinetic energy: \(E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\)
  - Gravitational potential energy: \(E_g = mg\Delta h\)
  - Cellular respiration: \(C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{energy}\)
  - Photosynthesis: \(6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2\)
  - Law of conservation of energy: energy is transferred or transformed, not created or destroyed.

- Top 5 Common Error Areas:
  - Confusing energy transfer with energy transformation.
  - Treating “lost” energy as destroyed instead of transformed into less useful forms such as thermal or sound energy.
  - Confusing chemical bond energy transformations with nuclear energy transformations.
  - Confusing radiation, conduction, convection, greenhouse gases, and greenhouse effect.
  - Listing only benefits of energy technologies without evaluating risks, waste, safety, and public trust.

## Chapter 4.4: How do we use the big bang theory to explain what we know about the universe?

### 4.4 Concept 1: The big bang theory is based on two main sets of evidence: redshift and cosmic background radiation — p.354–358

- [ ] KP1: Big bang theory
  - Core idea/formula: The big bang theory explains the origin of the universe, how it began, how it has changed, and how it is changing.
  - Key application: Use the theory to connect evidence about expansion with the history of the universe.

- [ ] KP2: Cosmology
  - Core idea/formula: Cosmology is the study of the origin, structure, development, and possible future of the universe.
  - Key application: Recognize the big bang theory as part of Western scientific cosmology.

- [ ] KP3: Doppler effect
  - Core idea/formula: The Doppler effect occurs when waves from a moving source are compressed or stretched.
  - Key application: Use changes in light wavelengths to infer motion of stars and galaxies.

- [ ] KP4: Redshift
  - Core idea/formula: Redshift occurs when light from an object moving away is stretched toward longer, redder wavelengths.
  - Key application: Interpret redshifted galaxy spectra as evidence that galaxies are moving away from us.

- [ ] KP5: Blueshift
  - Core idea/formula: Blueshift occurs when light from an object moving toward an observer is shortened toward bluer wavelengths.
  - Key application: Interpret blueshifted spectra as evidence that an object is moving toward the observer.

- [ ] KP6: Hubble’s law and expansion
  - Core idea/formula: Hubble’s law shows that a galaxy’s speed away from Earth is proportional to its distance from Earth.
  - Key application: Use redshift-distance patterns as evidence that the universe is expanding.

- [ ] KP7: Cosmic microwave background radiation
  - Core idea/formula: Cosmic microwave background, or CMB, radiation is leftover radiation from the big bang that fills the universe.
  - Key application: Use CMB radiation, including COBE and WMAP observations, as evidence that the universe was once much hotter and denser.

## Top 10 Core Formulas Across the Course

1. Complementary DNA base pairing: \(A \leftrightarrow T\), \(G \leftrightarrow C\)
2. DNA information flow: DNA \(\rightarrow\) RNA \(\rightarrow\) protein
3. Human chromosome organization: 46 chromosomes \(= 23\) pairs \(= 22\) autosome pairs \(+ 1\) sex chromosome pair
4. Mechanical kinetic energy: \(E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\)
5. Gravitational potential energy: \(E_g = mg\Delta h\)
6. Specific heat capacity: \(Q = mc\Delta T\)
7. Cellular respiration: \(C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{energy}\)
8. Photosynthesis: \(6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2\)
9. Hubble’s law pattern: \(v = Hd\)
10. Light-year distance: \(1\,ly \approx 9.46 \times 10^{12}\,km\)

## Top 10 Common Error Areas Across the Course

1. Confusing nucleotide, DNA, gene, allele, chromosome, chromatin, genome, and protein.
2. Confusing genotype with phenotype.
3. Treating Punnett square probabilities as guaranteed outcomes.
4. Saying individual organisms evolve instead of populations changing over generations.
5. Changing chemical subscripts instead of coefficients when balancing equations.
6. Confusing ionic compounds, covalent compounds, molecules, and formula units.
7. Confusing energy transfer with energy transformation.
8. Treating “lost” energy as destroyed instead of transformed into less useful forms.
9. Confusing radiation, conduction, convection, greenhouse gases, and greenhouse effect.
10. Confusing constellations, asterisms, galaxies, star clusters, solar systems, nebulae, redshift, blueshift, dark matter, and dark energy.
